# Iron & Ether — Vision Document

_What this game IS, what it does well, and how to build it._

---

## The Three Things We Do Really Well

Everything in the game serves exactly three pillars. If an idea doesn't serve one of them, cut it.

### 1. The Wand Discovery Loop
### 2. The Living World
### 3. The Betrayal You Participated In

That's it. Every encounter, every NPC, every map screen, every quest exists to serve one or more of these three.

---

## Pillar 1: The Wand Discovery Loop

**The core fantasy:** "I wonder what happens if I try THIS."

This is the game's heartbeat. Three slots, hundreds of combinations, and the player's constant desire to experiment is what keeps them moving through the world. Every new essence drop, every new form, every new behavior creates a burst of "let me try this with what I already have."

### How It Drives Everything

**Encounters exist to give you new toys.** Every enemy type drops a specific component. The player learns this. They see a new enemy and think "I need what that thing has." Combat isn't an obstacle between story beats — combat IS the progression system. You fight because fighting gives you new options.

**Spell Fusions are the treasure chests.** In Zelda, the moment you open a chest and get the hookshot, your brain lights up with every place you can NOW reach. Spell Fusions do the same thing for combat. Dragon's Teeth isn't just damage — it's a new way to think about every future encounter. "What if I use the skeleton soldiers as a distraction while I set up landmines?"

**Quests point you toward combinations.** NPCs don't say "go get the Dragon's Teeth fusion." They say things that make you think. Dagonet rambles about bats possessing other creatures. Dinadan complains about lightning bouncing between iron pillars. The player connects the dots: "Wait — Shotgun + Possess + Dark? Let me try that." The quest is the hint. The discovery is the reward.

### How to Plan an Encounter

Every combat encounter should answer ONE question: **what does the player learn here?**

- A room full of Skeleton Archers teaches the player that Bone essence exists and what it does
- A room where fire enemies stand in water teaches the player that essences interact with the environment
- A boss that uses Surtr's Crown (Orbit + Bloom + Cinder) teaches the player that THIS COMBINATION EXISTS by showing it to them before they can do it themselves

**Rule: Every boss uses a Spell Fusion the player can eventually learn.** The boss is a preview. The player thinks "I want to do THAT." Then they spend the next three hours figuring out how.

### Manufacturing Attachment to the Faerie's Sigh

You're right that players don't get attached to a weapon they receive in the first five minutes. Attachment comes from **investment and identity** — when the weapon feels like an extension of the player's personality.

**How the Sigh earns attachment:**

1. **It grows with your decisions.** Every player's Sigh is different because every player chooses different loadouts. By mid-game, a player who loves Seed + Bounce + Bone has a completely different relationship with the gun than someone running Lance + Linger + Cinder. The weapon IS your playstyle. Your identity.

2. **NPCs comment on YOUR loadout.** Not generic dialogue — specific reactions to what you're running. Palamedes sees you using Dark essence: "Careful with that one. It sees you back." Dagonet sees you using Shotgun: "Is that — are you shooting MULTIPLE things at once? That's CHEATING." This makes the weapon feel noticed, which makes it feel real.

3. **Visual transformation is YOUR transformation.** The Altered Beast progression (0 slots → 3 slots) means the player's appearance reflects their weapon choices. You don't just USE the Sigh — you BECOME it. When Morgan breaks it and you lose your visual transformation, you feel naked. Not because a cutscene told you to feel sad, but because your character looks wrong now. You've been staring at your powered-up form for 20 hours and now it's gone.

4. **The rebuilding.** After the Sigh breaks, you don't get a new weapon. You rebuild THIS one. Same weapon, your choices again, but now it responds differently — new charge sound, slightly different particle color, the iron frame has cracks that glow. It's the same companion, scarred, choosing you back. THAT'S when attachment peaks — not when you receive something, but when you almost lose it and get it back changed.

**The attachment isn't to "the Faerie's Sigh" as a narrative object. It's to YOUR build, YOUR playstyle, YOUR visual identity. Morgan breaking the weapon is Morgan breaking your save file.** That's personal in a way no cutscene can manufacture.

## Pillar 2: The Living World

**The core fantasy:** "This place existed before I got here and it'll keep going when I leave."

### The Elden Ring + Zelda 1 Formula (How to Actually Do It)

The question isn't "how do we make Elden Ring" — it's "how does a single-screen Zelda 1 world feel alive and discoverable?"

**Zelda 1 gives you the structure.** Grid of screens, each one a self-contained space. Walk to the edge, screen transitions. Dungeons are themed areas. Shops and NPCs are scattered. The player builds a mental map. This is non-negotiable — it's the skeleton of the game.

**Elden Ring gives you the philosophy.** The world has STUFF HAPPENING that doesn't involve you. NPCs have their own problems. Factions are fighting each other whether you show up or not. The story is discoverable, not delivered.

**How to combine them in a top-down single-screen game:**

**1. Screens tell micro-stories without words.**
Every screen is a scene. Not just terrain — a composition that implies something happened or is happening.

- A screen with three knight corpses arranged in a triangle around a scorched crater. What happened here? The player might never know, but the world feels real.
- A screen where Wyldwood vines are halfway through consuming a stone wall. The Fae are spreading. You can SEE it.
- A screen with a merchant cart, but no merchant. Broken wheel. Blood trail leading off-screen to the north. Do you follow it?

**2. The world changes based on what you've done.**
After you activate a Leyline Anchor (Morgan's instruction), the surrounding 3-4 screens should visibly change. More Wyldwood. Fewer friendly NPCs. An NPC who was there before is gone, or dead, or changed. The player notices. The game doesn't tell them why — they figure it out. This is your "player complicity" moment, and it's MORE effective than Spec Ops because the player connects the dots themselves.

**3. NPCs move.**
Dagonet isn't always in the same spot. Dinadan roams. Safir carries messages between zones. Palamedes' camp has different visitors depending on when you show up. When you return to a screen you visited 5 hours ago, something is different. Someone new is there, or someone is gone.

This is the Elden Ring feeling in Zelda 1's body. The world isn't static. It's a war zone, and war zones change.

**4. Multiple paths, gated by curiosity, not keys.**
The player should have 2-3 zones available at most times. Not "go anywhere" (too unfocused), not "go here next" (too linear). The Zelda 1 overworld had 128 screens, but most players explored maybe 60-70 before finishing. The rest was optional. That's the right ratio — 60% of the world is the critical path, 40% is reward for the curious.

NPCs hint at optional areas. Dagonet marks secret screens on the map. Dinadan complains about a place he refuses to go (which means you should go there). Brunor's quests send you to the hardest 10% of the map.

### NPC Permadeath

**Yes.** Some NPCs can die permanently if you fail to protect them or take too long.

**Rules:**

- **Core 4 NPCs (Palamedes, Dinadan, Dagonet, Brunor) CANNOT die.** They're too important to the game's structure. Losing your primary merchant or quest givers would break progression. These four are the load-bearing walls.
- **Secondary NPCs CAN die.** Tristan dies if you don't heal him in time. Tor dies if his escort quest fails. Pelleas remains enchanted forever if you never free him. Marhaus is already dead (environmental).
- **Death is permanent and acknowledged.** If Tristan dies, his body stays where it fell. Other NPCs mention it. "Heard about Tristan. Shame. He had intel on Cerdic's flanks — gone now."
- **Death locks content.** Tristan's death means no relic, no Cerdic intel, and no Arondight's Shadow fusion. That's the cost. The player who lost Tristan has a different game than the player who saved him. Dark Souls does this with Siegward, Greirat, Anri — die and their questlines vanish.
- **The player isn't told about time limits.** No countdown timer. No "hurry!" quest markers. The world moves forward. If you spent too long in the Wyldwood and Tristan bled out in the Iron March, that's the consequence of your choices. You might not even know you missed him.

**NPCs who can die and what it costs:**

| NPC | How They Die | What You Lose |
|-----|-------------|---------------|
| Sir Tristan | Bleed out in Iron March if not healed within ~2 zones of availability | Cerdic intel, relic, Arondight's Shadow fusion |
| Sir Tor | Killed during escort quest if you fail | Tor as camp ally, his late-game quest chain |
| Sir Pelleas | Wanders into Wyldwood permanently if not freed before Act III | Ring reward, Mistletoe's Chains fusion |
| Sir Yvain | His lion dies if you take too long on the fire-drake quest (Yvain lives, but leaves) | Dragonbreath fusion, future beast-hunt quests |
| Dame Ragnelle | Curse kills her if not broken before a certain story trigger | Yggdrasil's Root quest, Arachne's Loom fusion, late-game lore |
| Brother Caradoc | Cathedral falls to corruption if you don't complete Bors' quest chain | Free healing, blessed items, Green Man's Embrace fusion |

**The emotional angle:** Permadeath makes the world feel real. If everyone waits around forever for you, the war is a backdrop. If people die while you're somewhere else, the war is real. The player who saves everyone had a richer, easier game. The player who lost people had a harder, more personal one. Both are valid.

### Dagonet's Dragon Scene (First or Second Encounter)

After Dagonet joins the game, one of his early appearances has him standing proudly next to a dead creature in a clearing. He's posed heroically, one foot on it, addressing Morien as if recounting an epic battle.

> "MORIEN! You're just in time. You JUST missed it. A DRAGON. Enormous. Teeth like swords. I slew it with a single thrust — right through the heart. It barely had time to—"

The camera (or screen composition) reveals the "dragon" is a foot-long newt. It's not even dead — it's sleeping.

> "—it put up a tremendous fight. I'm actually still shaking. Do you want to buy its scales? Very rare. Fireproof."

He sells you "Dragon Scales" for an absurd price. They're just scales. They do nothing. Or — and this is the trick — one of the "useless" items in his junk inventory turns out to be a component for a Spell Fusion he doesn't know about. The joke has a gameplay payoff 10 hours later.

## Pillar 3: The Betrayal You Participated In

**The core fantasy:** "Wait — I did this?"

### What Morgan's Betrayal Actually Is

It's NOT "you were the bad guy all along" (Spec Ops). The combat is real. The enemies genuinely want to kill Morien. The war is happening. Morien isn't wrong to fight.

The betrayal is: **the person who armed you was using your survival as a data-gathering operation.** Every essence Morien absorbed mapped the Leyline network for Morgan. The gun was a sensor, Morien was the delivery system, and the enemies he killed were the power source.

This is a betrayal of TRUST, not morality. The player doesn't feel guilty — they feel used. And that's worse, because guilt implies you had a choice. Being used means you never did.

### How to Seed It Without Spoiling It

The player should feel something is "off" before the twist, but never be able to name what.

**Environmental seeding (no dialogue needed):**
- After activating the second Leyline Anchor, one screen that was previously safe now has Wyldwood corruption on its edges. Just visual. No quest marker. No NPC saying "the anchor did this."
- After the third anchor, a friendly NPC's shop screen is abandoned. They're gone. Where'd they go?
- The gun occasionally pulses when you're near an anchor — a different color than your current essence. The HUD hiccup is subtle. Most players won't notice on first playthrough.

**NPC seeding (natural dialogue, not exposition):**
- Palamedes, mid-conversation about something else entirely: "...the Leylines are shifting. Someone is mapping them. Be careful what you feed that weapon."
- Dinadan, in his usual cynical way: "Funny how Morgan gave you the one weapon that gets stronger the more you kill. Almost like she WANTS you fighting."
- Dagonet, accidentally: "I heard Morgan's voice once. In the Wyldwood. She was talking to someone about 'the readings.' I assumed she meant books. Do sorceresses read? They probably read."

None of these should flash a "PLOT IMPORTANT" flag. They're buried in conversation. The player who pays attention might feel uneasy. The player who doesn't will be blindsided. Both are valid experiences.

### How Story Integrates With Run-and-Gun (No Speech Bubbles)

You asked the right question. How does a run-and-gun game tell its story without stopping gameplay?

**The answer: the world tells the story. Morien doesn't.**

Morien is a near-silent protagonist. He doesn't monologue. He doesn't reminisce. He acts. The story is told through:

**1. What you see on each screen.**
A screen IS a story beat. Knight corpses near an anchor = the anchor killed them. Wyldwood vines covering a village = the merger is spreading. A lone NPC standing where a group used to be = people died or fled. The player reads this instantly, without stopping.

**2. What NPCs say when you visit them.**
You walk up, they talk. Short dialogue — 2-4 lines max for casual encounters. They reference things you've done, things that happened in the world, things other NPCs said. "Brunor went east. Haven't seen him since." "The swamp's gotten worse since you hit that anchor." This is your story delivery mechanism.

**3. What changes between visits.**
The player returns to a screen they cleared 3 hours ago. It's different. New enemies, or new corruption, or a new NPC, or a dead body. The change IS the story. No words needed.

**4. Boss dialogue (the ONE place the game stops).**
Boss introductions get 1-3 lines of dialogue. Boss deaths get 1-2 lines. These are the game's cutscenes — 10 seconds max. Aglovale's revelation ("She used you, boy. The weapon — it was never yours—") happens mid-fight, in a speech bubble during a phase transition. The fight doesn't stop. You're processing the story while dodging attacks. That's integration.

**5. Item descriptions.**
Every relic, every essence, every Spell Fusion has a 1-2 sentence description in the menu. This is your lore delivery. The player reads it when THEY want to, during a natural pause (checking inventory, deciding loadout). Not forced, not interruptive.

**The Brunor/Dragon's Teeth memory:** When the player uses Dragon's Teeth in the Bone Fields where they got the quest, the screen could have a unique visual — the skeletons react differently, or there's a small environmental detail (Brunor's sword stuck in a rock nearby, from his previous attempt). The world remembers. Morien doesn't need to say anything.

## The Belonging Theme (From Planescape, Done Subtly)

You asked how to convey different philosophies of belonging without being heavy-handed. The answer: **you don't convey them. You let the player experience them.**

Each NPC treats Morien differently, and the player FEELS the different philosophies without anyone naming them:

**Palamedes** greets Morien with respect from the first meeting. Doesn't mention his race, his outsider status, nothing. Just treats him like a knight. Sells him good gear. Gives tactical advice. The player feels: "This guy sees me as an equal." That's belonging through competence — Palamedes earned his place and extends the same standard to Morien. No dialogue about it. Just behavior.

**Dagonet** treats Morien like a friend immediately because Dagonet treats EVERYONE like a friend. He doesn't care about status, race, knighthood — he needs someone to talk to and you're here. The player feels: "This guy would befriend a rock." That's belonging through performance — Dagonet belongs because he makes people laugh, and he offers Morien the same unconditional acceptance.

**Dinadan** is dismissive at first. Sarcastic. "Oh good, another errand knight." But his quests keep getting more personal. By his 10th quest, he's saying "I need YOU specifically for this one." The player feels: "I earned this." That's belonging through proving yourself — Dinadan doesn't give respect freely, so when he does, it means something.

**Brunor** asks nothing and offers everything. His quests are the hardest in the game but he doesn't pressure you. "There's a fire giant. I can't kill it. Maybe you can. Maybe you can't." No judgment either way. The player feels: "This guy just... believes in me?" That's belonging through sincerity — Brunor's acceptance is so genuine it's almost disorienting.

**Kay** is openly hostile. Dismissive of Morien. "Another of Morgan's pets." Rude, territorial, makes you feel unwelcome. The player feels: "Screw this guy." But Kay guards Camelot. Kay is loyal to Arthur above all. And if Morien proves himself, Kay — GRUDGINGLY — gives him Rhongomyniad's recipe. The hardest single encounter in the game. Kay didn't change. He just acknowledges strength. That's belonging through power — the only language Kay speaks.

**Morgan** was warm. Protective. Gave Morien the weapon, guided him in dreams, called him special. The player felt belonging through being chosen. Then the betrayal reveals it was manipulation. The warmest belonging was the false one. That hits without ever saying the word "belonging."

**The player experiences all of these naturally through gameplay.** Nobody gives a speech about outsiders. Nobody says "the theme of this game is belonging." The player just... feels differently around different NPCs. And by the end, they know which version of belonging they value most, because they know which NPC they sought out when they needed help.

## How to Plan a Zone

Each zone needs:

### 1. An Identity (2 words max)
- **The Ashlands:** scorched war
- **Whispering Swamp:** drowning life
- **Wyldwood:** beautiful danger
- **Drowned Valley:** frozen memory
- **Iron March:** desperate invasion
- **Fractured Cathedral:** broken faith

### 2. A New Enemy Ecosystem (3-5 enemy types)
Each zone introduces at least one new enemy type that drops a component the player doesn't have yet. This drives the discovery loop — entering a new zone means new toys.

### 3. A Story Thread (told through screens)
The zone should tell a story through its screen compositions. Not narrated, not quest-texted. Just: what does this place LOOK like, and what does that tell you?

The Ashlands: screens progress from "active battle" (soldiers fighting, fortifications) to "aftermath" (corpses, craters, abandoned camps) to "no man's land" (nothing alive, just scorched earth). The player walks through a war's timeline by moving east.

### 4. At Least One NPC Encounter
Someone who lives here, or is passing through, or is dying here. They anchor the zone as a place where people exist, not just monsters.

### 5. One Secret (hidden screen, hidden NPC, hidden path)
Reward for exploration. A screen you reach by walking into a seemingly solid wall. A path behind a waterfall. A NPC who only appears at night (if we implement time).

### 6. One Quest Hook
Something that starts here but might finish elsewhere. Or something that started elsewhere and finishes here. Zones connect through quests, not just geography.

## How to Plan a Dungeon

### Structure: 15-25 screens per dungeon

**Entrance screens (2-3):** Establish the vibe. Light enemies, environmental storytelling, the dungeon "introduces itself."

**Exploration screens (8-12):** Keys, locked doors, branching paths, enemy gauntlets. The meat. Each path teaches the player something about the dungeon's identity. One path might be heavy Cinder enemies (hinting at the boss's fire weakness). Another might have environmental puzzles.

**Pre-boss screen (1):** A quiet screen. A save point, a merchant (maybe Dagonet hiding in a ridiculous spot), an NPC who gives one line of context. The calm before the storm. Zelda does this perfectly — the empty room before the boss door.

**Boss arena (1-2 screens):** The boss uses a Spell Fusion the player can learn. The arena design teaches the player how to fight by constraining their options. A narrow arena for a wide boss = dodge focus. A large arena for a fast boss = positioning focus.

**Treasure room (1):** Post-boss reward. New form, new behavior, new essence, relic, or Spell Fusion recipe. The hookshot moment — "everything just changed."

### Each Dungeon Has a Design Thesis

Not a theme — a thesis. What does this dungeon TEACH the player?

- **Dungeon 1 (Bone Crypt):** Teaches that enemies telegraph their drops. Bone enemies = Bone essence. The dungeon is all Bone/Shatter enemies. By the end, the player understands the ecosystem.
- **Dungeon 2 (Drowned Aqueduct):** Teaches that environments interact with essences. Water conducts Pulse. Sap enemies heal near water. The player must think about WHERE they fight, not just how.
- **Dungeon 3 (Burning Keep):** Teaches that some enemies are resistant to what they drop. Cinder enemies resist Cinder attacks. The player must use a DIFFERENT essence to kill them, incentivizing loadout variety.

Each dungeon is a lesson. By the final dungeon, the player has learned everything they need.

## How to Plan a Quest

### Quest Formula: HINT → JOURNEY → DISCOVERY → REWARD

**Hint:** An NPC says something. Not "go here and do this" — an observation, a complaint, a rumble, a lie (Dagonet). The player thinks "I should check that out."

**Journey:** Travel to the location. The travel itself should involve 2-3 screens of relevant encounters. The journey IS content, not filler between the quest-giver and the objective.

**Discovery:** The quest objective teaches the player something. A Spell Fusion they didn't know existed. A lore detail that reframes an earlier event. A hidden NPC. An enemy type they haven't seen. The objective should surprise.

**Reward:** A Spell Fusion recipe, a relic, a component, map intel, or an NPC alliance. Something that changes how the player plays going forward.

### Quest Quality Tiers

**Dinadan quests (15-20):** Quick, sharp, funny. 1-3 screens of travel, a combat encounter or puzzle, done. The writing carries these. Dinadan's descriptions of the objective are the highlight. Reward: Fusion components, recipes, map reveals.

**Brunor quests (8-10):** Long, hard, sincere. 4-6 screens of travel through dangerous territory, a multi-phase fight or gauntlet, real stakes. No humor — pure earnest challenge. Reward: Best relics, rarest Fusions, unique components.

**Dagonet quests (5-8):** Accidental. Dagonet didn't mean to give you a quest — he was just rambling. But the information he dropped is real and the location he described exists. Reward: Hidden areas revealed on map, junk with hidden value, Fusion hints.

**One-off NPC quests (10-15):** Self-contained short stories. Yvain's lion, Pelleas' enchantment, Ragnelle's curse, Tristan's wound. Each is its own miniature narrative with a moral dimension — not "should I do this quest" but "how do I handle this situation?"

## What the Game Feels Like to Play

You boot it up. Chaos. Knights dying. You're handed a weapon. Five minutes in, you're shooting things and it feels good.

Thirty minutes in, you've found your first essence and the weapon changed color. You try shooting and the projectiles are different. You think "what if I combine this with that other thing?" You spend ten minutes just experimenting in a safe screen.

Two hours in, you've explored 20 screens, found Palamedes (he's useful), met Dagonet (he's hilarious), and accidentally stumbled into a dungeon. You beat the boss. You got Bolt form. Your weapon fires completely differently now. The discovery loop has you.

Five hours in, you've activated two Leyline Anchors because Morgan told you to. The world seems fine. You found your first Spell Fusion by accident and it was incredible — the screen lit up, the name appeared, you felt like you'd found buried treasure. You saved Tristan (or you didn't, and you don't know what you missed). Dinadan gave you three quests and his dialogue made you laugh.

Ten hours in, something's wrong. The screens near the first anchor look different. Darker. NPCs are scared. Palamedes said something cryptic about the weapon. You push forward because the combat loop is too good to stop and there's a Fusion you're trying to complete.

Fifteen hours in, Aglovale. Your father. Corrupted. He tells you the truth mid-fight. The weapon breaks after. Your character looks different — diminished. Your damage drops. The components you worked for are gone. You feel it in your hands because the gameplay changed, not because a cutscene told you to feel sad.

Twenty hours in, you've rebuilt. The weapon has cracks that glow. The new charge sound is deeper. You're earning everything back, but it feels different this time — heavier, more deliberate, YOURS.

Twenty-five hours in, Dagonet steps out. The jester. The fraud. He can't fight. He does it anyway. You get through because of him. You circle back and he's quoting Arthur and making jokes about cheese and he's alive and you're never going to forget this character.

Thirty hours. The end. Parzival. The one character you've seen in the distance the whole game, never spoken to. He turns. He speaks one line. He gives you Excalibur's Light. You use it to win. The gun breaks for real. Mordred watches from a hill. Credits.

The player sits there thinking about Morgan. About whether she was right. About Dagonet's courage. About the weapon they spent 30 hours building that chose them back. About the screens they visited that changed because of what they did.

That's the game. Run and gun on the surface. Everything else underneath.
